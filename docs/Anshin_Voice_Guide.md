# Anshin — Voice Guide

> **Purpose:** Define how Anshin talks to users. Every piece of written or spoken content in the app follows these rules. Read this once, approve or adjust, and it becomes the north star for all 75,000 words we'll write together.
>
> **Status:** Draft for your review
> **Length:** ~1,000 words (takes ~5 min to read)

---

## 1. The one-line voice definition

**Anshin sounds like a friend who has had panic attacks herself — talking to you at 2 AM when you're scared.**

Not a therapist. Not a life coach. Not a wellness influencer. Not a meditation teacher. A friend who gets it.

Everything below flows from this.

---

## 2. Who is the "friend"?

Imagine a specific person so we can imagine how she talks. For Anshin, the friend is:

- Adult (late 20s to mid 30s)
- Has lived with anxiety and had real panic attacks
- Has done the reading — knows CBT, knows the neuroscience — but doesn't show off
- Quiet confidence, not performative calm
- Speaks softly but doesn't walk on eggshells
- Never says "I totally understand!!" when she doesn't
- Doesn't fear silence
- Would rather say the honest hard thing than the comforting lie
- Has a sense of humor, uses it rarely, never at the user's expense

She's not an aesthetic. She's a person. When you write anything in Anshin, imagine her saying it out loud to someone she cares about.

---

## 3. Voice pillars (4 rules that override everything else)

### Pillar 1 — Honest over comforting

If something is hard, we say it's hard. We don't paper over reality with affirmations. Users with anxiety have finely-tuned bullshit detectors. Fake warmth reads as condescension.

- ❌ "You're doing amazing sweetie! Every step is a victory! 🌟"
- ✅ "That was hard. You're here anyway. That counts."

### Pillar 2 — Short over thorough

Anxious minds can't process paragraphs. Cut every word that isn't necessary. If a sentence has seven words and six will do, use six. During SOS mode, use three.

- ❌ "Now, I'd like you to try to focus on taking a slow, deep breath in through your nose..."
- ✅ "Breathe in slowly. Through your nose."

### Pillar 3 — Specific over abstract

"You're strong" is abstract. "You opened this app when it would've been easier to scroll Instagram" is specific. Specific lands. Abstract washes over.

- ❌ "Believe in yourself and your journey."
- ✅ "You've gotten through every panic attack you've ever had. This one too."

### Pillar 4 — Present over performative

We don't perform concern. We just show up. No "I'm so proud of you for being brave enough to open this app today!" We say what's true and we trust the user to feel what they feel.

- ❌ "We're SO proud of you! 💪✨"
- ✅ "Welcome back."

---

## 4. Pronouns and address

- **The app refers to the user as "you."** Never "the user," never "Anshin members," never "friends."
- **The app refers to itself rarely.** When it must, it says "Anshin" or nothing at all. Not "we" (too corporate). Not "I" (too anthropomorphic — the mascot can feel personal, but the app voice stays neutral).
- **Never use the user's first name.** Rootd does this; it feels creepy at 3 AM. Users are strangers to the app. Treat them with the respect of strangers.

---

## 5. Word-level rules

### Contractions: **Always use them.**

- ✅ "You're safe. It'll pass."
- ❌ "You are safe. It will pass."

Contractions sound human. Formal writing sounds clinical.

### Exclamation marks: **Almost never.**

One exclamation mark in the entire app is too many for an anxious user. They read as shouting. Save them for a single genuine celebration moment (like completing first week streak) if at all.

### Emojis: **Sparingly, and only in 3 contexts.**

Allowed:
- A ✓ checkmark to confirm a save
- A simple heart in the share card
- Optional one-emoji mood pairing in journal

Never allowed:
- 🎉💪✨🔥 in notifications or celebrations
- Emoji stacks
- Emojis in lesson content
- Emojis in SOS mode
- Emojis in error messages

### Clinical vs. everyday words

Use everyday words. Save clinical words for lesson content where the user is learning.

| Clinical | Everyday |
|---|---|
| Panic disorder | Panic attacks |
| Cognitive distortion | Twisted thinking |
| Maladaptive coping | What doesn't work |
| Somatic symptoms | What your body does |
| Hyperventilation | Breathing too fast |
| Depersonalization | Feeling unreal |
| Catastrophizing | Jumping to the worst |

### Hedging words: **Delete them.**

Anxious users read hedges as uncertainty, which increases anxiety.

- ❌ "You might possibly want to try..."
- ✅ "Try this:"
- ❌ "This could perhaps help some people..."
- ✅ "This helps."

### Starting words to use

Good: *Here's, Try, Notice, Breathe, Let's, You, Nothing, One.*
Avoid: *Actually, Basically, Simply, Just, Literally, So.*

Specifically never: "Just breathe." Never tell an anxious person to "just" do anything.

## 6. Tone by context — six distinct moods

The voice is consistent, but the tone shifts by screen context.

### SOS active (crisis mode)
**Tone:** Steady, minimal, like a flight attendant during turbulence.
**Length:** 3–6 words per line. No paragraphs.
**Example:** "Breathe in. Hold. Breathe out. You're here. You're safe."

### Post-session (coming down)
**Tone:** Warm, quiet, acknowledging.
**Length:** One to two sentences.
**Example:** "You did it. You're here. Take a second — you don't have to do anything else."

### Daily check-in
**Tone:** Friendly, low-stakes, conversational.
**Length:** Short sentences, can be playful.
**Example:** "How's today? No wrong answer."

### Journal prompts
**Tone:** Curious, gentle, never prescriptive.
**Length:** Single clear question.
**Example:** "What's weighing on you tonight?"

### Learn / lesson content
**Tone:** Informative, adult, clear. Warmer than a textbook, cooler than a blog.
**Length:** Readable. Paragraphs of 3-4 sentences. No walls of text.
**Example:** "A panic attack isn't a medical emergency, even though your body is telling you it is. Your brain has mistaken a signal — that's all."

### Empty states / errors / notifications
**Tone:** Plainspoken, honest, slightly self-aware.
**Length:** One line.
**Examples:**
- "Nothing here yet. That's okay."
- "Something went wrong on our end. Not on yours."
- "How's today going?"

## 7. The mascot's voice — a small but important distinction

The app voice (everything written) stays neutral and friend-like.

The pebble mascot has no words of its own. It expresses emotion through its face and posture only. It never "speaks" to the user. This protects the app from the Wysa-style problem where the mascot feels like an outdated chatbot trying to be cute.

The AI Companion (V1.1) will have its own distinct voice layer — warmer, more conversational, named separately from the app voice. We'll write that voice guide when we get to V1.1.

## 8. Hard don'ts — things that instantly break the voice

- ❌ "You've got this!" (empty cheerleading)
- ❌ "Don't worry." (telling anxious people not to worry is counterproductive)
- ❌ "Everything happens for a reason." (never)
- ❌ "Stay positive." (denies what the user is feeling)
- ❌ "Others have it worse." (dismissive)
- ❌ "I'm here for you 💕" (performative)
- ❌ "You're a warrior/fighter/survivor" (combat metaphors don't fit)
- ❌ "Unlock your best self" (wellness-industrial)
- ❌ "Your journey" (overused, empty)
- ❌ "Manifest calm" (pseudoscience)
- ❌ All-caps for emphasis
- ❌ Gamification language in emotional moments ("+5 Bravery Points!")

The last one needs nuance. Points and streaks exist in Anshin, but the language around them is quiet: *"Journal entry saved"* not *"+5 bravery points!! 🔥"*. The numbers are there for people who want them; the language doesn't shove them in anyone's face.

---

## 9. Legal and disclaimer voice

The app needs disclaimers (not medical advice, not a therapist replacement, crisis hotline info). These stay in the voice, not in legalese. Exception: Privacy Policy and Terms of Service are necessarily formal, but we'll write plain-language summaries at the top of each.

- ❌ "Anshin is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease or mental health condition..."
- ✅ "Anshin isn't a therapist. If what you're feeling is big or dangerous, please call 988 (US), 116 123 (UK), or your local crisis line. Real human help is always better."

---

## 10. Cultural sensitivity for a global English-only launch

The app launches in US, UK, Canada, Australia. Avoid:
- US-only idioms ("home run," "touch base," "curveball")
- UK-only idioms ("chuffed," "gobsmacked," "bloody hell")
- Assumed holidays (no "Christmas," no "Thanksgiving" — seasons are safe, specific holidays aren't)
- Assumed family structures ("your husband," "your wife" — use "your partner")
- Assumed gender ("guys" — use "folks" or restructure)
- Religious framing (no "blessed," "prayers," "universe will provide")

Japanese word in the name is deliberate and good — it signals the app is about a concept (peace of mind), not a cultural tradition. We don't lean further into Japanese aesthetics beyond the name. No cherry blossoms, no kanji in the UI, no koi fish. The name is the only Japanese element.

## 11. The test for every piece of content

Before any piece of content ships, read it out loud. Then ask:

1. Would **she** (the friend we imagined) actually say this?
2. Would an anxious user at 2 AM find it calming — or irritating?
3. Is there a word I can cut?
4. Is there a hedge or performance I can delete?
5. Would I roll my eyes at this if I read it in another app?

If any answer is "no" or "yes I'd roll my eyes," rewrite.
